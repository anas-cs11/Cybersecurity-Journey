# 1. The Goal: The CIA Triad
Every security plan is designed to protect three core pillars, known as the CIA Triad. If a risk affects any of these, the organization is in trouble.

Confidentiality: Keeping sensitive data private (e.g., ensuring only you can see your bank balance).

Integrity: Ensuring data is accurate and hasn't been tampered with (e.g., preventing someone from changing the amount in your bank account).

Availability: Making sure systems and data are accessible when needed (e.g., ensuring you can log into your banking app 24/7).

## 2. The "What, Why, and How" of Security
To build a plan, security teams analyze the relationship between three elements: Assets, Threats, and Vulnerabilities.

Assets (The "What")
An Asset is anything of value that an organization wants to protect.

Examples: Customer data, employee laptops, intellectual property, or even the physical office building.

Priority: Not all assets are equal. An analyst must decide which assets are "mission-critical" and deserve the most protection.

Threats (The "Why")
A Threat is any potential event that could negatively impact an asset.

Human Threats: A hacker (malicious) or an employee who accidentally deletes a file (unintentional).

Environmental Threats: A flood that destroys a server room or a power outage.

Vulnerabilities (The "How")
A Vulnerability is a specific weakness or flaw that a threat can exploit.

Examples: An outdated software version, a weak password, or a server room door that doesn't lock properly.

## 3. The Security Mindset: A Practical Example
Think about the windows in your home.

Asset: The window itself (it has value and keeps the elements out).

Threat: A severe storm with high winds.

Vulnerability: A small crack in the glass or a loose frame.

Risk: The storm (threat) exploits the crack (vulnerability), causing the window (asset) to shatter, which compromises the Availability of your home's protection.
