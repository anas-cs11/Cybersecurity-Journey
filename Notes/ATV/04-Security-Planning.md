# 1. The Hierarchy of Security Planning
While the terms are often mixed up in casual conversation, in a professional security environment, Policies, Standards, and Procedures have very distinct roles.
| **Document Type** | **Function** | **Focus** | **Analogy: Driving a Car** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Policy** | **Strategic:** High-level rules and goals. | The "What" and "Why" | "We must drive safely to protect lives." |
| **Standard** | **Tactical:** Mandatory requirements/benchmarks. | The "How Well" | "The speed limit is 60 km/h." |
| **Procedure** | **Operational:** Step-by-step instructions. | The "How To" | "Turn the key, check mirrors, shift to Drive." |

## 2. Policies: The Rules of the Road
Policies are the foundation. They establish the organization's stance on security and are often legally binding.

Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): This is one of the most common policies. it tells employees exactly what they can and cannot do with company equipment (e.g., "Do not use work laptops for personal gambling sites").

## 3. Standards: The Measuring Stick
Standards ensure consistency. Without them, one department might think a 4-digit password is fine, while another requires 16 characters.

External Standards: Many companies adopt standards from expert bodies like NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) or ISO.

Internal Standards: A company might set a standard that all data at rest must be encrypted using a specific algorithm (like AES-256).

## 4. Procedures: The Manual
Procedures are the "boots on the ground" documents. They are designed so that any authorized person can follow them to get a predictable result.

Example: A "Password Reset Procedure" would list exactly which internal website to visit, which identity verification questions to ask, and how to confirm the reset was successful.

## 5. Why the "People" Factor is the Biggest Risk
Technology rarely fails as often as humans do. Human Error is a leading cause of security breaches.

The Scenario: An employee uses their personal email to send a "Confidential" file because the company file-share was acting slow.

The Security Response: By having a signed Policy (AUP) and a clear Procedure for secure file sharing, the organization creates Accountability. If a breach happens, the analyst can point to the specific step in the procedure that was skipped.
