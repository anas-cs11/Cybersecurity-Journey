**The IEEE 802.11 StandardIEEE 802.11** is the technical name for what we commonly call **Wi-Fi**. It is a suite of protocols that define how communication happens on a **WLAN (Wireless Local Area Network)**.

- **IEEE:** Stands for the **Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers**. This is the global organization that creates and maintains these technical standards.
- **802.11:** This specific number refers to the group of protocols used for wireless local networks.

---

**Wireless Security Protocols (WPA)**
Because wireless signals travel through the air, they are easier to intercept than wired connections. To solve this, **WPA (Wi-Fi Protected Access)** was created to provide encryption and authentication.

The standard has evolved through three major versions to stay ahead of hackers:

| **Version** | **Introduction** | **Security Level** | **Key Features** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **WPA** | 2004 | Basic | Replaced the older, weaker WEP (Wired Equivalent Privacy). |
| **WPA2** | 2004/2006 | Strong | Uses stronger encryption (AES) and is the current standard for most home and office networks. |
| **WPA3** | 2018 | Toughest | The newest version. It provides much stronger protection against password-guessing attacks and better encryption for public Wi-Fi. |

**The Role of a Security Analyst**
In a professional organization, a security analyst must ensure that all wireless access points are configured correctly. This usually involves:

- **Disabling older protocols:** Making sure devices don't "downgrade" to weak security like WEP or the original WPA.
- **Enforcing WPA2/WPA3:** Ensuring that only the most modern encryption standards are used to protect the company's data.
- **Monitoring Access:** Keeping track of who is connecting to the **WLAN (Wireless Local Area Network)** to ensure no unauthorized devices are present.
