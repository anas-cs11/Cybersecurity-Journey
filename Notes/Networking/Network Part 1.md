## 1. Defining the Modern Network

A network is defined as a **group of connected devices**. Understanding this structure is the prerequisite to understanding the importance of securing it.

## The Scope of Connectivity

Devices on a network can communicate with each other over two primary mediums:

- **Network Cables:** Physical wired infrastructure.
- **Wireless Connections:** Cordless data transmission.

## Device Categorization by Environment

The transcript highlights that networks exist in various settings, involving different hardware:

- **Home Networks:** Includes laptops, cell phones, and **Smart Devices** (specifically **refrigerators** and **air conditioners**).
- **Office Networks:** Includes **workstations**, **printers**, and **servers**.

## 2. Identification and Communication

For communication to occur, devices must be able to **find each other** and establish a connection. This is managed through unique identifiers that ensure data reaches the **right device**.

| **Identifier** | **Full Name** | **Purpose** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **IP Address** | **Internet Protocol** Address | A unique address used to locate and identify a device on a network. |
| **MAC Address** | **Media Access Control** Address | A unique hardware identifier for a specific device. |

## 3. Network Classification: LAN vs. WAN

Networks are differentiated by their geographical span and how they facilitate communication between different locations.

## LAN - Local Area Network

- **Definition:** Spans a **small area** such as a home, an office building, or a school.
- **Example:** When a personal device (like a **cell phone or tablet**) connects to the **Wi-Fi** in your house, they form a LAN.
- **Connectivity:** The LAN then connects to the internet.

## WAN - Wide Area Network

- **Definition:** Spans a **large geographical area** such as a city, state, or country.
- **The Internet:** The transcript defines the **Internet** as one big WAN.
- **Function:** It allows networks in one location to communicate with networks and devices in other locations

## 4. Real-World Context: Global Resource Sharing

**Case Study: The San Francisco to Dublin Link**
Consider a company with employees in **San Francisco** and **Dublin, Ireland**.

1. **The Need:** The employee in San Francisco needs to communicate and **share resources** with the colleague in Ireland.
2. **The Medium:** Because of the vast distance, this communication happens over a **WAN**.
3. **The Result:** Using **unique addresses** (IP and MAC), the data bypasses millions of other devices to land specifically on the Dublin employee's workstation.
