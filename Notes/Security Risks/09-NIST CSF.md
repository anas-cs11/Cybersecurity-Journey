## **The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)**

The **CSF** is a voluntary set of standards and best practices used by all types of businesses (for-profit, nonprofit, and government) to manage risk. It is recognized globally as the gold standard for security.

## **The 5 Core Functions of CSF**

These functions provide a step-by-step cycle for handling any security event:

1. **Identify:** Find and understand the systems, people, and risks involved.
2. **Protect:** Put safeguards in place to ensure services keep running.
3. **Detect:** Spot a security event as soon as it happens.
4. **Respond:** Take action once a threat is detected.
5. **Recover:** Restore any capabilities or services that were damaged.

---

## **Real-World Example: The "Infected Phone" Scenario**

Here is how an analyst uses the CSF when a workstation is compromised by a USB device:

- **Identify:** You get an alert and find the specific laptop with an unknown device plugged in.
- **Protect:** You remotely block the device to stop the threat from spreading.
- **Detect:** you remove the laptop and use tools to see if the hacker did anything else.
- **Respond:** You investigate! You find out an employee was just charging an infected phone in a laptop USB port.
- **Recover:** You fix the damaged files and clean the workstation so it can be used again.

## **Special Publication: NIST SP 800-53**

While the CSF is for everyone, **SP 800-53** is a specific "rulebook" for the **U.S. Federal Government**.

- **Purpose:** To provide a unified set of security controls for federal information systems.
- **Scope:** It covers government agencies AND private companies that do work for the government.
- **Goal:** To strictly maintain the **CIA Triad** (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability) for high-stakes government data.
