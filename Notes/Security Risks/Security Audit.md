## **Security Audits: The Ultimate Check-up**

A **Security Audit** is a formal review of an organization's security "health." It compares current controls and policies against a set of expected standards (like NIST or OWASP).

## **1. Two Main Types of Audits**

- **External Audit:** Conducted by an outside organization (often for legal or regulatory reasons).
- **Internal Audit:** Conducted by the organization's own team (Compliance Officers, Managers, and Analysts).
    - **Goal:** To find weaknesses, avoid fines, and improve **security posture** before a real threat (or an external auditor) finds them.

---

## **2. The First Steps of an Internal Audit (Planning)**

### **A. Defining Scope and Goals**

- **Scope:** The "boundaries" of the audit. What exactly are we checking?
    - *Includes:* Specific people, office locations, software, and procedures.
- **Goals:** What the organization wants to achieve.
    - *Example:* "We want to ensure all our user permissions follow the Principle of Least Privilege."

### **B. Conducting a Risk Assessment**

- **Definition:** Identifying the specific **threats, risks, and vulnerabilities** the organization faces right now.
- **Analyst Role:** You might be asked to look at the "raw data" of a risk assessment to see which controls (like passwords or firewalls) are missing.
    - *Example:* A risk assessment might find that company laptops (physical assets) aren't being tracked properly, or that the internal network (digital asset) has weak login rules.

---

## **3. Why This Matters for Entry-Level Analysts**

Even if you don't *start* the audit, you will likely be the one:

1. **Reviewing the Scope:** To understand which systems you need to monitor.
2. **Analyzing the Risk Assessment:** To understand which "holes" in the defense you need to help plug.
3. **Providing Evidence:** Showing that current controls (like encryption) are actually working.
