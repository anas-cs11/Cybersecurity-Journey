## The Big Three: Threats, Risks, and Vulnerabilities
To understand these, think of a bank vault:

The **Threat** is a bank robber.

The **Vulnerability** is a door left unlocked.

The **Risk** is the chance that the robber finds the unlocked door and steals the money.

**1. What is an Asset?**
Simply put, an **Asset** is anything the company owns that has **value**.
• **Physical:** Laptops, office buildings, servers.
• **Digital:** Customer names, credit card info (PII), or "Top Secret" company recipes (Intellectual Property).

**2. Threats (The "Who" or "What" that causes harm)**
A threat is any person or event that could hurt your assets.
• **Social Engineering:** Tricking people into giving up secrets.
    ◦ *Analogy:* A stranger pretending to be a repairman to get into a building.
• **Phishing:** The most common threat. It's a fake email (like from a bank) designed to steal your password.

**3. Vulnerabilities (The "Weak Spot")**
A vulnerability is a **hole in your defense** that a threat can use to get inside.
• **Technical Weakness:** Using an old version of Windows or a very easy password like "123456."
• **Human Weakness:** Employees who haven't been trained to spot a fake email.
• **Physical Weakness:** A server room door that doesn't lock properly.

**4. Risk (The "Chance" of something bad happening)**
Risk is the **probability** (how likely it is) that a threat will actually succeed in using a vulnerability.

**Risk Levels Table**

Organizations sort risks into three levels to decide what to fix first:

| **Risk Level** | **Impact** | **Example** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Low** | No real damage to money or reputation. | Someone leaking the company’s public lunch menu. |
| **Medium** | Could cause some money loss or embarrassment. | Leaking a company's internal goals before they are ready. |
| **High** | Huge disaster; legal trouble; loss of millions. | A hacker stealing thousands of social security numbers (PII). |
|  |  |  |

## **How Security Analysts Help (The "Action Plan")**

- **Education:** Train staff so they don't become a "human vulnerability."
- **Access Control:** Use ID cards and passwords so only the right people get in.
- **Monitoring:** Keep an eye on systems and encourage staff to report anything that looks "fishy."
